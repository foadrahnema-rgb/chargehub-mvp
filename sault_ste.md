<h1>Sault Ste. Marie → Thunder Bay EV Charging Corridor</h1>

<h2>Why this corridor matters</h2>
<p>This stretch of Northern Ontario along the Trans-Canada Highway (Lake Superior shoreline) is one of the most discussed long-distance routes for both gas and EV drivers.</p>
<p>Large distances between towns, exposure to weather, and limited charging redundancy mean a <strong>single charger going down can turn a routine drive into a high-stress situation</strong>. Even experienced EV drivers often describe this route as <em>"doable, but risky"</em> if anything unexpected happens.</p>
<p>This corridor view is designed to focus on <strong>optionality</strong> — where you still have choices, and where a stop becomes a <strong>single point of failure</strong>.</p>
</div>

<div class="section">
<h2>Corridor overview</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Route:</strong> Sault Ste. Marie → Thunder Bay (Trans-Canada Hwy / Hwy 17)</li>
  <li><strong>Typical concern:</strong> 200+ km gaps between reliable fast-charging options</li>
  <li><strong>Primary anxiety trigger:</strong> Knowing <em>this</em> charger must work</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="section">
<h2>Charging sequence (high-level)</h2>
<p><em>Distances are approximate and vary by exact charger location and route deviations.</em></p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Sault Ste. Marie</strong><br>Multiple fast-charging options. Last truly redundant stop before long gaps begin.</li>
  <li><strong>Wawa area</strong> (~225–230 km)<br>Critical stop. Limited alternatives. If unavailable, options become severely constrained.</li>
  <li><strong>Marathon / Terrace Bay area</strong> (~185–210 km)<br>Another long segment where weather, speed, and elevation matter.</li>
  <li><strong>Nipigon area</strong> (~105–115 km)<br>Redundancy improves slightly, but earlier failures still cascade here.</li>
  <li><strong>Thunder Bay</strong><br>Multiple fast-charging options and urban redundancy.</li>
</ol>
<p class="legend">Segments ≥200 km are where single-point-of-failure anxiety typically spikes.</p>
</div>

<div class="section">
<h2>Why anxiety spikes here</h2>
<ul>
  <li><span class="warning">Single-point-of-failure risk:</span> Several segments exceed 200 km with no practical fast-charging alternatives. If a charger is down, busy, or inaccessible, there may be no nearby fallback.</li>
  <li><span class="warning">Dynamic conditions:</span> Cold fronts, wind off Lake Superior, rain, and snow can significantly reduce real-world range.</li>
  <li><span class="warning">Psychological load:</span> Even with sufficient battery, knowing <em>there is no Plan B</em> creates stress — the same feeling drivers describe as “this one has to work.”</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="section">
<h2>How experienced drivers reduce risk on this route</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Treat key stops as <strong>mandatory</strong>, not optional</li>
  <li>Arrive with <strong>larger buffers</strong> than usual</li>
  <li>Avoid optimizing charging speed at the expense of optionality</li>
  <li>Check <strong>recent uptime reports</strong> before committing</li>
</ul>
<p>Experience helps — but <strong>network awareness helps sooner</strong>.</p>
</div>

<div class="section">
<h2>What this corridor view is for</h2>
<p>This page is not about maximizing speed or minimizing stops.</p>
<blockquote><em>If something goes wrong at the next charger, do I still have options?</em></blockquote>
<p>On routes like this, that question matters more than range on paper.</p>
